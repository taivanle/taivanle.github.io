Engineering AI Governance Workflows in a Regulated Environment

Nationwide watsonx.governance Pilot. Turning policy into executable workflow logic with enforced gates evidence capture and audit ready progression.

Focus: governance workflows, risk tiering logic, lifecycle gates, traceability, platform integration

Back to Projects

Context

As AI adoption accelerates in financial services governance cannot remain a static review layer. It must be engineered directly into the AI lifecycle.

I worked on a governance focused AI pilot for Nationwide using watsonx.governance. The objective was to convert manual risk and compliance processes into structured system enforced workflows that reduce ambiguity and increase auditability.

The core question

How do you convert policy risk frameworks and regulatory obligations into executable logic?

My role

I owned the translation layer between governance intent and workflow execution. That meant taking policy language and converting it into deterministic rules that control progression through the lifecycle.

I focused on designing the structured inputs that drive governance decisions. I then modelled those decisions as conditional workflow paths with mandatory checkpoints evidence capture and controlled state transitions.

  • Designed metadata schemas to represent governance attributes as machine readable fields
  • Implemented risk tier classification logic mapped to downstream controls
  • Modelled conditional workflow paths with progression gates
  • Defined validation checkpoints for documentation evidence and approvals
  • Shaped audit ready state transitions aligned to review stages

From policy to workflow logic

Financial institutions operate under layered risk frameworks covering model risk data protection operational risk and regulatory compliance. These frameworks usually live in documentation. Documentation is not enforceable logic.

The build treated governance as a control system. Instead of relying on reviewers to interpret requirements manually we encoded requirements into workflow behaviour. This shifts enforcement from human judgement to system constraints while retaining explainability.

Risk tiering and conditional controls

A key component of the pilot was implementing risk tier scoring logic. Projects were assessed through structured inputs including:

  • Model type and use case
  • Data sensitivity classification
  • Customer impact scope
  • Regulatory exposure

These inputs drove risk tier assignment which triggered downstream controls automatically. Higher risk tiers required deeper evidence capture and more rigorous approval pathways.

  • Higher risk classifications triggered DPIA workflows automatically
  • Specific data categories required additional documentation uploads
  • Certain model types required enhanced approval pathways

This was implemented using conditional workflow logic that controlled state transitions and progression gates. Governance became system driven rather than reviewer dependent.

Embedding evaluation and traceability

Beyond workflow gating we focused on traceability and auditability. The design enforced structured evidence capture at defined lifecycle stages with requirements linked directly to risk tiers.

  • Enforced evidence capture before progression at key lifecycle boundaries
  • Linked documentation requirements to risk tiers for consistency
  • Maintained versioned checkpoints across approval states
  • Created deterministic progression paths through the lifecycle

Evaluation criteria were embedded into workflow steps so model documentation risk assessments and control evidence were captured before progression. This reduced ambiguity in decision making by constraining allowable state transitions based on governance criteria.

Architecture

The pilot was implemented as a governed workflow layer that orchestrated document processing evidence capture and lifecycle progression. Orchestration and enforcement lived in watsonx Orchestrate. Enterprise content services ran on OpenShift.

MCP tools provided controlled interfaces to downstream services. Watson Document Understanding converted unstructured content into structured metadata aligned to governance schemas. FileNet persisted enterprise records. Postgres supported metadata storage. Object Storage handled durable artefacts.

High level integration view

Orchestration enforced governance logic. MCP tools abstracted platform services. WDU extracted structured metadata. FileNet and Postgres maintained enterprise persistence plus audit traceability.

Technical considerations

From a technical standpoint the complexity was not in code volume. It was in modelling correctness. The system needed to be strict enough to prevent risk leakage while flexible enough to support iterative development.

  • Ensured risk logic remained interpretable plus explainable
  • Avoided overly rigid workflows that slowed delivery
  • Maintained alignment between policy language plus executable conditions
  • Designed escalation paths that reflected real world review hierarchies

Outcome

  • Structured automated governance workflows
  • Reduced reliance on manual tracking plus email based approvals
  • Improved audit traceability across AI initiatives
  • Clear alignment between risk classification plus control enforcement

More importantly the pilot demonstrated that governance can be engineered as infrastructure. Policy becomes executable constraint logic rather than guidance reviewed after the fact.

Reflection

One key lesson was that AI governance is fundamentally a systems design problem. It requires formalising ambiguity encoding policy into deterministic logic designing controlled progression paths and balancing risk mitigation with operational velocity.

As models become more capable organisations that scale responsibly will treat governance as an engineered capability embedded in execution layers rather than an afterthought.
